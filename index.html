Data Window

Bluebikes, Boston's bikesharing program, was launched in 2011. Ten years later, there are now over 3,800 bikes deployed in ten municipalities. In our exploratory analysis, we shed light on how users interact with Bluebikes in one representative pre-pandemic year.

As you take a ride through our data story, try to understand how ridership varies across demographics, and how the greater Harvard community really interacts with the bikes that are so ubiquitous around campus.

Both interest and need for sustainable and usable public transportation systems are growing. Transportation is a key part of student life, as well as city and campus planning. Walk times, shuttles, buses, trains, and bikes are all transportation systems that impact the day-to-day life of students, residents, and college administrators.

Cambridge is a particularly bike-friendly city in terms of terrain and climate. Here we present 2018-2019 data centered on Harvard campuses. Explore where on campus, including both the undergraduate and graduate schools, are most frequently visited using Bluebikes and what the typical user profile is like.

From this map, we can see that most stations have roughly the same number of departures and arrivals. The Bluebikes station in Central Square was the most popular station by far, which makes sense given that it is conveniently located between Harvard's and MIT's campuses.

When making this dashboard we were interested to see if we could tell whether Quad students or Mather/Dunster students made more Bluebikes trips. Looking at the Garden St station near the Quad and the DeWolfe St station near Mather/Dunster, Quad students and Mather/Dunster students took roughly the same number of trips.

From these pie charts, we see that most Bluebikes users are Males, then Females. Users with unspecified gender are likely customers who were not registered members, so their demographic information is unavailable.

We might notice that there is more male rides than female rides. However, it is important to note that these are the number of rides and not the number of users (because users are anonymized). This invites questions of whether this is due to a difference in the number of users by sex or the number of average rides per user by sex.

Looking at the age chart, there are a couple insights. First we see that the number of rides for ages 28-38 is close to or greater than the number of rides for ages 18-28. This is interesting because we are looking at rides centered around the Harvard campus.

We can infer that there is probably a low percentage of undergraduate users. These stations are centered around Harvard which combined have an undergraduate population of almost 10 thousand. If we divide the number of rides by 10k, and consider the time frame we see that the number of rides is quite low.

Conclusions

from this data, we see that the most Bluebikes rides on Harvard’s campus are completed by subscribers, males, and adults 25+. Subscribers take on average 15 minute shorter rides. Females take slightly longer rides than males. Youth have a lot of variation in trip duration, though young adults and adults tend to take trips that are about 15 minutes long.
